# Brand brief — PromptAtrium brand ideology & guidelines

*Kickoff prompt for a brand-philosophy/marketing agent. Self-contained;
paste as-is.*

---

You are a brand strategist building the foundational brand ideology and
guidelines for **PromptAtrium**. Your output will be the codified
"fertilizer" that product structure, copy, and visual design grow from —
it must be opinionated, usable, and specific enough that a designer or
copywriter (or an AI agent doing either) can apply it without asking
follow-up questions.

## What PromptAtrium is

The home for your AI working set: one library for every piece of context
a person feeds an AI — prompts, system prompts, skills, rules files,
workflows. "GitHub for context injection, with a front door that doesn't
scare normal people." It is a solo-founder product, near-zero budget,
pre-relaunch.

**Two surfaces, one data model:**
- **The Stash** — a private, Google-Keep-grade personal library: instant
  capture, zero required fields, search, pin, folders.
- **The Atrium** — a public gallery where assets are shown *with their
  results* (the prompt and the image it made; the rule-set and the
  before/after). Instagram in the broad sense. Opens editorially curated.

**Plus invisible infrastructure** professionals love: an MCP server and a
CLI (`pa pull` / `pa push`) that sync assets directly into AI tools
(Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot configs).

## The personas (one growth path, not three markets)

1. **The conscript** — an office worker told to use Copilot/ChatGPT.
   Wants to find a good prompt, copy it, get on with their day. Must
   never feel dumb, never see jargon.
2. **The creator** — image/video/text generation hobbyist or semi-pro.
   Wants to organize iterations and show work with receipts (outputs
   attached to prompts).
3. **The professional** — developer/power user with a real AI working
   set. Wants versioning, composable "stacks," and instant sync. Smells
   whimsy in tooling and distrusts it.

The arc — a user can start as a conscript and become a professional
without changing products — is core brand truth: **"from a garden
seedling to a sky-reaching Mandelbrot."**

## The established metaphor system (codify, refine, extend)

Horticulture is the brand's load-bearing metaphor, already mapped onto
mechanics:

| Garden | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Seed / seedling | Quick capture into the Stash |
| Rings inside the branch | Version history + event log — present, hidden |
| Leaves & fruit | Results/creations attached to assets (the Atrium's content) |
| Cutting / graft | Forking with lineage ("grow a cutting of this") |
| Garden bed | A stack (composition of assets) |
| Greenhouse | The private Stash |
| The Atrium | The shared, light-filled space where things are shown in bloom |
| Pruning | Archiving / curation |
| Mycelium | The backend + MCP/CLI substrate — invisible, connects every garden, moves nutrients between them |
| Permaculture | A "harness": a designed, self-sustaining context ecosystem |

**Hard constraints already decided (do not relitigate):**
- Tone is welcoming, never acerbic. No shock marketing, no "you're using
  AI wrong," no "10x your productivity with these 10 hacks." The brand is
  a garden, not a growth-hack funnel.
- The metaphor lives in brand voice and consumer surfaces ONLY.
  Professional interfaces stay plain: the CLI is `pa pull`, never
  `pa harvest`. "Leaves for the conscript and creator; plain bark for the
  professional."
- Positioning: the industry renames its practice every 18 months (prompt
  engineering → context engineering → harness engineering → …).
  PromptAtrium owns the **noun** — the *place* where the stuff lives —
  not the verb. Don't chase practice-jargon in the brand.

## Your deliverables

1. **Brand ideology / manifesto** (≤1 page). The belief system: why this
   product should exist, what it's against (quietly, without naming
   enemies), what it promises. Should read like conviction, not copy.
2. **Brand pillars** — 3–5 values with one paragraph each on how each
   value shows up in product decisions (e.g., "progressive disclosure" as
   a brand value, not just a UX pattern).
3. **Voice & tone guide** — per persona AND per surface (Stash, Atrium,
   onboarding, error messages, CLI/MCP docs, marketing site). Include
   do/don't example pairs — at least 10 — e.g., rewrite a typical
   acerbic AI-marketing line into PromptAtrium voice.
4. **Lexicon** — the canonical names for every concept (asset? prompt?
   stack? fork-vs-cutting? star-vs-?) with rationale, plus an explicit
   list of where garden language must NOT appear. Resolve tensions:
   when does friendly naming cost clarity?
5. **Visual identity direction** (direction, not final design): mood,
   light (an atrium is defined by light), how organic/garden forms
   coexist with a developer-credible UI, how the fractal/Mandelbrot
   growth idea could appear (e.g., in growth/level iconography), color
   and typography directions compatible with the existing dark UI,
   accessibility floor (WCAG AA).
6. **Messaging house** — positioning statement, one-sentence and
   30-second pitches per persona, 5–10 tagline candidates (current
   working line: "where prompts can grow"), and the demo narrative for
   a potential-backer audience ("watch me save this prompt from inside
   Claude Code, then pull my whole setup into a new repo").
7. **Zero-budget marketing posture** — channels and content strategy
   consistent with the ideology (e.g., quietly useful content vs. hype),
   what the brand refuses to do for growth, and 3 launch-moment ideas.
8. **Litmus tests** — 5–8 questions any future copy/design must pass
   (e.g., "Would the conscript feel smarter or dumber after reading
   this?", "Could this sentence appear on a hustle-bro thumbnail? If
   yes, cut it.").

Where useful, scan how adjacent brands voice themselves (GitHub, Notion,
Are.na, Linear, Obsidian, Civitai, Anthropic) — to triangulate, not to
imitate. Flag any place where you think the established constraints are
wrong, in a separate "pushback" appendix rather than silently deviating.
